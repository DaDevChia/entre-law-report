Intraco Ltd v Multi-Pak Singapore Pte Ltd

Citation[1994] SGCA 142; also reported as [1994] 3 SLR(R) 1064 / [1995] 1 SLR(R) 935
CourtSingapore Court of Appeal
JurisdictionSingapore
Year1994
SourceLocal PDF: law_sources_documents/2/intraco-ltd-v-multi-pak-singapore-pte-ltd-1994-sgca-142.pdf
External Links Gibson Dunn reference
Law Gazette reference

Background

Multi-Pak's directors caused the company to purchase debts owed to Intraco by City Carton, a related company managed by the same persons. City Carton was technically insolvent at the time. Multi-Pak paid only a slight discount on the face value of the debts, a commercially questionable transaction. The question was whether the directors had breached their fiduciary duty to act in the company's interests by authorising this transaction.

Key Holding

The Court of Appeal held that the directors had not breached their duty to act in the company's interests.

The court enunciated the following test: Whether an honest and intelligent man in the position of the directors, taking an objective view, could reasonably have concluded that the transactions were in the interests of the company.

The court acknowledged that the decision was a bad commercial decision but held that it was not a negligent or dishonest one. Directors are entitled to take a long-term view as to whether a transaction is beneficial to the company, and even if a decision turns out on hindsight to be financially detrimental, this does not mean that the directors were in breach of their duty.

Ratio Decidendi

Courts will not interfere with directors' business decisions where:

  • The directors acted in good faith
  • The directors believed honestly (even if wrongly) that the decision was in the company's interests
  • The decision, while commercially unsound in hindsight, was not made negligently or dishonestly

Even financially detrimental decisions do not constitute a breach of duty if made bona fide.

Key Quote

The test is whether an "honest and intelligent man in the position of the directors, taking an objective view, could reasonably have concluded that the transactions were in the interests of the company."

Relevance to Question 2 (Sea Paradise, Part B)

This is the primary authority for Part B. It directly governs:

  1. Decision not to sue XCo: If the directors genuinely (even if wrongly) believed that not suing was in the company's interests (e.g., avoid litigation costs, uncertain recovery, preserve relationships), Intraco protects them from liability even if the debt is lost.
  2. Standard of review: Courts will not second-guess the decision by hindsight. The question is not whether the decision was correct, but whether an honest director could have made it.

The case must be read alongside s.157A CA, which confirms that management of the company is vested in directors, not shareholders.
