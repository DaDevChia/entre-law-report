Ho Kang Peng v Scintronix Corp Ltd (formerly known as TTL Holdings Ltd)

Citation[2014] SGCA 22; also reported as [2014] 3 SLR 329
CourtSingapore Court of Appeal (Sundaresh Menon CJ; Chao Hick Tin JA; V K Rajah JA)
JurisdictionSingapore
Year2014
SourceLocal PDF: law_sources_documents/7/Ho Kang Peng v Scintronix Corp Ltd 2014.pdf

Background

TTL Holdings Limited (later renamed Scintronix Corp Ltd) was a publicly listed company on the SGX, engaged in the manufacture and supply of moulds and finished plastic components. Ho Kang Peng was the CEO from 1 November 2005, executive director from 24 November 2005, and executive chairman from 23 November 2007. He stepped down as CEO and executive chairman on 28 March 2008 but remained as a non-executive director until 23 October 2008.

Ho signed a "Bontech Agreement," a consulting agreement with Bontech Enterprise Co Ltd (a Taiwanese company), on behalf of the Company on 1 August 2006. The agreement was for unspecified "consulting services" with no Schedule A (which was supposed to set out the scope of services). No services were in fact rendered by Bontech. Despite this, the Company made eight payments totalling S$169,644.97 to Bontech, all signed off by Ho via payment vouchers, without formal Board resolution or approval. No invoices or receipts were issued by Bontech. The payments allegedly were handed over to a Shanghai-based director, Oh Chye Huat, who passed them to an individual known as "Mr Lee" in exchange for his undertaking to procure business from Pioneer Technology (Shanghai) Co Ltd.

Key Holdings

The Court of Appeal held that Ho had breached his duty to act in the interests of the company.

The court applied a subjective test with an objective element:

  • The primary question is whether the director honestly believed, in his own mind, that the decision was in the company's interests (subjective).
  • However, the court will also examine, objectively, whether an honest director in that position could have held such a belief.
  • If no reasonable director could have believed the decision was in the company's interests, the court will find that the director did not in fact hold that honest belief, as the subjective test is informed by an objective check.

The court found that no honest director could have believed that making undocumented payments for unverifiable services was in the company's interests.

Ratio Decidendi

The duty to act in the company's interests is assessed by a subjective test with an objective element: courts examine not only what the director claimed to believe, but whether an honest director could reasonably have held that belief.

Key Principle

A director who claims to have acted in the company's interests must show that an honest director in his position could have believed that the impugned conduct was for the benefit of the company.

Relevance to Question 2 (Sea Paradise, Part B)

Critical for evaluating both impugned decisions in Part B:

  1. Not suing XCo: The directors would need to show they honestly believed this was in the company's interests, AND that an honest director could have held that view. If the debt is large and clearly recoverable (given Mr X signed knowing XCo was insolvent, potentially fraudulent trading), the objective limb may not be satisfied.
  2. Interest-free loan to Director C: No honest director could have believed that granting an interest-free loan to a fellow director (in violation of CA s.162) was in the company's interests. The Ho Kang Peng objective check would fail.
