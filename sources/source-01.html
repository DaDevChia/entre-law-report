Over and Over Ltd v Bonvests Holdings Ltd and Another

Citation[2010] 2 SLR 776
CourtSingapore Court of Appeal
JurisdictionSingapore
Year2010
SourceLocal PDF: law_sources_documents/6/Over & Over Ltd v Bonvests Holdings Ltd and another 2010.pdf

Background

Richvein Pte Ltd was incorporated on 20 August 1980 as a joint-venture company between two families: the Sianandar family (through Unicurrent Finance Limited, holding 70%) and the Lauw family (through Over & Over Ltd ("O&O"), holding 30%). Richvein's sole business was the Sheraton Towers Singapore hotel on Scotts Road. The relationship between the families was informal and based on mutual trust; despite the Lauws' minority position, the Sianandars had agreed to an "Important Decision Term": that the Lauw family would be consulted on any important decisions relating to Richvein's operations and management.

The relationship deteriorated when Henry Ngo ("HN"), who controlled Bonvests (which later acquired Unicurrent's shareholding), engaged in several acts without consulting O&O: (1) entering into related-party transactions with companies in which HN had substantial interests (HIHR for hotel management, Colex Holdings for waste disposal, IPM for cleaning services) without board ratification or O&O's consent; (2) engineering a rights issue at $0.38 per share (designed for "maximum dilution" of O&O's stake) when the net asset value per share was over $1.06; and (3) repeatedly breaching the Important Decision Term.

Key Holding

The Court of Appeal (V K Rajah JA delivering the judgment) found that the majority had abused their voting powers in a manner that was commercially unfair to the minority. The court affirmed the broad scope of s.216 of the Companies Act and the underlying criterion of commercial unfairness.

The court held that:

  • The test for oppression is commercial unfairness: a departure from the standards of fair dealing and a violation of the conditions of fair play which a shareholder is entitled to expect.
  • Abuse of voting powers by a majority shareholder, to benefit themselves at the expense of the minority, constitutes oppression.
  • The court has wide remedial powers under s.216(2), including ordering a buy-out.

Ratio Decidendi

The abuse of majority voting powers to unfairly prejudice minority shareholders constitutes oppression under s.216 of the Companies Act. Commercial unfairness, not illegality, is the operative standard.

Relevance to Question 2 (Sea Paradise, Part A)

  • Establishes the commercial unfairness standard as the governing test under s.216.
  • Relevant where the majority directors/shareholders have used their position (and control over dividend declarations and loan approvals) to benefit themselves at the expense of the minority.
  • The insertion of Articles 10, 11, and 12 (which entrench director positions and limit shareholder powers) and the subsequent failure to pay dividends could be characterised as an abuse of majority power to prejudice minority shareholders.
