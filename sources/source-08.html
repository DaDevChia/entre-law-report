Re Smith and Fawcett Ltd

Citation[1942] Ch 304
CourtEngland and Wales (Court of Appeal)
JurisdictionUnited Kingdom
Year1942
SourceLocal PDF: law_sources_documents/8/8. Re Smith and Fawcett Ltd.pdf

Background

Smith & Fawcett Ltd was incorporated on 15 December 1937 as a private company. Its nominal capital was 25,000l., divided into 10,000 preference and 15,000 ordinary shares of 1l. each, but only 8,002 ordinary shares were issued: 4,001 to Joseph Fawcett and 4,001 to Norman Smith, the two directors. Article 10 of the articles of association provided: "The directors may at any time in their absolute and uncontrolled discretion refuse to register any transfer of shares."

Fawcett died on 26 February 1940, bequeathing 2,000 shares to his daughter Alice Marjorie Fawcett and 2,001 shares to the plaintiff, his son Edwin Arthur Fawcett (the executor). The plaintiff applied to register the shares. Smith refused to consent to registration of all shares, offering instead to register only 2,001 shares and to buy 2,000 at a price fixed by himself. Smith then appointed his solicitor, J. W. Feather, as a second director. The plaintiff applied to the court by way of motion to rectify the register. Simonds J. dismissed the motion, and the plaintiff appealed.

Key Holding

The Court of Appeal (Lord Greene M.R.) held that where articles confer a discretion on directors regarding transfer of shares, directors must exercise their discretion bona fide in what they consider, not what a court may consider, is in the interests of the company, and not for any collateral purpose.

Lord Greene M.R.'s formulation:

"They must exercise their discretion bona fide in what they consider, not what a court may consider, is in the interests of the company, and not for any collateral purpose."

The court also held that there is nothing, in principle or in authority, to prevent articles from conferring the widest possible power on directors to refuse transfers, including for reasons not personal to the transferee but bearing on the general interests of the company as a whole (e.g., whether admitting a new member would give that person too great a weight in the councils of the company).

This is the foundational statement of the subjective test for directors' duty to act in the company's interests: the standard is what the director honestly believes, not what an objective observer would conclude.

Ratio Decidendi

Directors must act bona fide in what they (the directors) consider to be the interests of the company. The court does not substitute its own judgment for that of the directors on commercial matters, provided the directors acted honestly.

Relevance to Question 2 (Sea Paradise, Part B)

This case provides the foundational authority for the subjective test applied in Singapore. It supports the directors' position in Part B:

  • Decision not to sue XCo: If the directors honestly (even if incorrectly) believed that pursuing litigation was not in the company's interests, Re Smith and Fawcett protects them.
  • Scope of judicial deference: The case establishes why courts are reluctant to interfere with business decisions: the test is the directors' honest belief, not the court's commercial judgment.
  • Note: The subjective test has been qualified in Singapore by the objective element added in Ho Kang Peng v Scintronix (Source [6]) and Intraco v Multi-Pak (Source [5]).
