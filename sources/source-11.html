"The Director's Duty of Care: A Comparative Analysis of Delaware and Singapore Laws"

Year1998

Summary

This academic article provides a comparative analysis of the director's duty of care in Delaware (United States) and Singapore.

Delaware Approach

  • Delaware courts apply the Business Judgment Rule (BJR): a strong presumption that directors made decisions in good faith, on an informed basis, and in the honest belief that the decisions were in the company's best interests.
  • Courts refuse to substitute their judgment for that of directors, provided the requirements of the BJR are met (informed, disinterested, independent).
  • This insulates directors from liability for business decisions.
  • Key Delaware cases analysed: Smith v Van Gorkom, In re Dataproducts, Canal Capital v French.
  • Delaware DGCL permits exculpation clauses (s.102(b)(7)), allowing companies to limit directors' monetary liability for duty of care breaches.

Singapore Approach

  • Singapore does not have a formally codified Business Judgment Rule (unlike Australia's Corporations Act 2001, s.180(2)).
  • However, Singapore courts effectively apply equivalent principles through the subjective test (Re Smith and Fawcett) with an objective element (Ho Kang Peng v Scintronix) and the Intraco v Multi-Pak principle (no liability for honestly-made decisions that turn out to be financially detrimental).
  • The duty of care standard under s.157(1) CA is objective but calibrated: a continuum depending on the director's role (Lim Weng Kee v PP).
  • Singapore courts are also reluctant to second-guess directors' business decisions, reflecting practical respect for the BJR without formal adoption.
  • CA s.172(1) makes exculpation clauses void in Singapore (unlike Delaware DGCL), meaning directors cannot contract out of liability for negligence.
  • s.391 provides a judicial relief provision, but it is stricter than Australia's equivalent (s.1318).

Executive vs. Non-Executive Directors

The article distinguishes between the standards applicable to executive and non-executive directors, a distinction also drawn in Daniels v Anderson (Australia) and Lim Weng Kee v PP (Singapore). NEDs are held to a lower standard given their limited involvement in day-to-day management, but retain a minimum obligation of care.

Key Recommendations

The article recommends that Singapore should adopt BJR-like protections to encourage directorial risk-taking and support the separation of ownership and control, while maintaining the objective standard of care as a minimum floor.

Relevance to Question 2 (Sea Paradise, Part B)

  • Provides academic support for the argument that Singapore courts approach the business judgment question in a manner functionally equivalent to the US BJR.
  • Supports the critical commentary required in Part B: the question asks whether courts should interfere more or less with directors' business decisions; this article's comparative analysis of Delaware and Singapore is directly on point.
  • Relevant to the discussion of whether Singapore should formally adopt the BJR (as Australia has) or continue with the current approach.
  • The article's discussion of the void exculpation clause (s.172(1) CA) is relevant to whether Singapore directors face greater personal liability risk than their Delaware counterparts, which in turn affects the policy question of judicial intervention.
