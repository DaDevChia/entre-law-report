Singapore Prestigious Club Membership Terms, Transferability, and Personal vs. Corporate Ownership Restrictions

TitleSingapore Prestigious Club Membership Terms and Covenants
TypeResearch Compilation (Multiple Club Sources)
DateMarch 2026
PurposeEvidence supporting legal argument that a company loan to a director for a "prestigious club membership" lacks genuine business justification

Research Purpose

To provide evidence supporting a legal argument that a company loan to a director for a "prestigious club membership" lacks genuine business justification, on the grounds that:

  1. Club memberships are personal assets belonging to the individual member, not corporate assets
  2. Memberships are subject to personal covenants restricting corporate ownership
  3. Guest policies and non-member access render the claimed business purpose (monitoring a restaurant in the club) unnecessary

Key Findings

1. Singapore Island Country Club (SICC)

Membership Classification

  • Ordinary Membership is "purchased by and registered under an individual's name"
  • Memberships are classified as Local Ordinary (for Singapore Citizens or Permanent Residents) or Foreign Ordinary (for non-residents)
  • Term Membership is available on a yearly basis: S$24,000 entrance fee per year, renewable yearly, non-transferable
  • Ordinary memberships can be purchased from the open market at listed prices (S$370,000 to S$900,000 market range as of 2024-2025)

Transfer Restrictions

  • Ordinary (Transferable) memberships can be transferred to another individual person, subject to General Committee approval
  • Term memberships are explicitly "non-transferable"
  • Long-service members: If a member has been a member for 20+ years and attains age 65, may transfer to a child/grandchild over 21 years without transfer fee
  • Individual memberships cannot be transferred to a company or corporation; transfer is restricted to individual persons only

Personal Covenants

  • Individual members must enroll as individuals; membership cannot be held in a corporate name
  • A Term Member "shall not be entitled to vote or hold office or be co-opted to serve on any Committee or Sub-Committee"
  • Individual membership is strictly personal; it is not a corporate asset that can be held on behalf of the company

Guest Policies

  • Members or their spouse may introduce any person as a Guest to use the Club's facilities
  • Guests must be accompanied by the member at all times
  • Members are responsible for their guest's conduct and unpaid expenses
  • Non-members can access the club as guests without full membership

Dining Facilities

SICC has multiple dining venues operated for member use:

  • Silk Chinese Restaurant (Teochew cuisine)
  • Oka Japanese Restaurant with teppanyaki service
  • Bistro @ SICC (fusion cuisine)
  • Multiple F&B outlets available to members

This demonstrates that a company seeking to use the club for entertaining purposes or conducting business could:

  1. Book the restaurant facilities without requiring a personal membership
  2. Have staff attend as guests of a member
  3. Arrange corporate entertaining without individual director membership

Costs

  • Ordinary membership: S$370,000-S$900,000 (market range, 2024-2025)
  • New Social membership tier (2025): S$138,888
  • Term membership: S$24,000/year
  • Monthly subscriptions vary by membership type

3. Singapore Cricket Club (SCC)

Membership Types and Personal Nature

  • Ordinary (Transferable) Membership: Individual membership that can be sold/transferred
  • Term Membership: One year, renewable up to 5 years
  • Corporate (Transferable) Membership: Registered under a company name; allows nomination of one or more individuals

Corporate Membership Details

  • Eligible applicants: "A registered company, firm or business in Singapore"
  • Cost: S$50,000 (plus GST) for 2 corporate nominees
  • Transfer fee for corporate membership: S$20,000 plus GST
  • Administrative fee for changing a nominee: S$5,000 (plus GST)
  • One corporate nominee may extend membership to spouse and unmarried children aged 16-21

Key Distinction

  • Individual memberships are personal; held by individuals with voting rights at General Meetings
  • Corporate memberships are corporate assets; held by the company with nominees as representatives
  • Only Honorary, Ordinary Members, and Corporate Nominees can vote at General Meetings

Facilities

  • Dining venues: Padang Restaurant (fine dining), Victoria Room (including specialty Indian cuisine)
  • Available for corporate functions and celebrations
  • Non-member access possible as guest of member or for specific corporate events

8. Tax and Legal Implications

Company Loans to Directors: Statutory Restrictions

Singapore Companies Act, Section 162: Loans made by a company to its director are prohibited with limited exceptions:

  1. Home Loan Exception: Loans to full-time directors/employees for home acquisition (one outstanding at a time); requires shareholder approval at General Meeting; must be repaid within reasonable timeframe
  2. Employee Benefit Scheme Exception: Part of approved employee benefit schemes; requires shareholder approval; must be sanctioned by General Meeting
  3. Ordinary Course of Business Exception: For companies in the business of lending money; subject to banking/finance/insurance regulations
  4. Failure to Obtain Approval: If approval is not obtained by the next Annual General Meeting, the amount must be repaid within 6 months

Tax Treatment of Club Membership Loans

IRAS Guidance on Benefits-in-Kind:

According to IRAS (Inland Revenue Authority of Singapore), club memberships provided to directors are taxable benefits:

  • As Employment Benefit: If the loan is provided to the director in their capacity as an employee, the interest benefit is taxable as employment income
  • Calculation of Taxable Benefit: Outstanding loan balance x Average Prime Lending Rate
  • Club subscriptions: Benefits-in-kind such as club memberships provided in place of cash are taxable
  • Subscription Deduction: Subscriptions and club entrance fees paid for sole proprietors, partners, or directors' personal memberships are not allowable as business expenses

Tax Treatment Depends on Capacity

Director Capacity (Employee):

  • Interest benefits qualify as taxable employment income
  • Loan is taxable to the director personally
  • Company cannot deduct as business expense

Shareholder Capacity:

  • Interest benefits are non-taxable
  • Requires documented evidence: repayment schedule in loan agreement, legitimate non-tax reasons
  • More difficult to establish genuinely

Critical Issue for Present Case: If a company grants a director an interest-free loan for a personal club membership:

  • The director is in their capacity as an employee/director
  • The membership is personal property, not a corporate asset
  • The interest benefit (entire amount, given 0% interest) is fully taxable to the director
  • The company cannot deduct the loan or its interest foregone as a business expense
  • The claimed business purpose ("entertain clients, watch restaurant") is undermined by the fact that non-members can access the restaurant as guests

9. Key Evidence Summary for Legal Argument

(a) Memberships are Personal Assets, Not Corporate Assets

Evidence:

  • SICC ordinary membership is "purchased by and registered under an individual's name"
  • SCC personal ordinary memberships are held by individuals with voting rights
  • Tower Club personal term memberships are explicitly "non-transferable" to the company
  • Tanglin Club memberships require sponsorship by individuals and are conferred on persons, not entities
  • Mandala Club memberships are individually registered

Conclusion: Club memberships across Singapore's prestigious clubs are fundamentally personal property held by the individual member. A company cannot hold an ordinary membership; corporate membership structures exist separately with nominated individual representatives.

(b) Guest Policies Demonstrate Alternative Access Routes

Evidence:

  • SICC: Members may introduce any person as a Guest; guests do not require membership
  • SCC: Non-members can access facilities as guests of members
  • Singapore Club: Members may introduce guests (limited to 5 occasions/year)
  • Tower Club, Mandala Club: Have non-member entertaining options

Conclusion: The claimed business purpose, enabling the director to "entertain clients" and "watch over the restaurant," can be achieved through guest policies without purchasing an expensive personal membership. The company could arrange corporate entertaining, non-member venue bookings, or have authorized personnel attend as guests.

(c) Membership Costs Demonstrate Personal Investment Value

Evidence:

  • SICC ordinary membership: S$370,000-S$900,000 (market range)
  • Sentosa Golf Club: S$500,000-S$830,000
  • Tower Club transferable: S$25,000+
  • SICC term membership: S$24,000/year
  • Mandala Club: S$5,500 + S$198/month
  • These represent significant personal assets with appreciating value in some cases

Conclusion: The magnitude of these costs suggests club memberships are treated as personal investments and status symbols rather than business tools. A company making such an expense for an employee appears to be providing a personal benefit rather than a business expense.

(d) Tax Treatment Confirms Personal Benefit Character

Evidence:

  • Interest-free director loans are taxable as employment benefits
  • Club subscriptions are not allowable business expenses
  • IRAS treats club memberships as benefits-in-kind to directors
  • No deduction available to the company

Conclusion: Singapore tax law explicitly treats club memberships as personal benefits to directors, not corporate deductions. This further supports the argument that the company loan lacks genuine business justification.

Relevance to Assignment Question 2(b)

This research supports the argument that the company's grant of an interest-free loan to Director C for a club membership represents a problematic business decision because:

  1. Legally Prohibited or Questionable: Under Singapore Companies Act s.162, director loans are prohibited except in narrow circumstances. A club membership does not clearly fall within the home loan, employee benefit scheme, or ordinary lending exceptions. Without shareholder approval, the loan may be illegal.
  2. Taxable Benefit to Director: The interest benefit (entire S$X amount at 0% interest) is taxable to the director as employment income under IRAS guidelines, creating a tax liability the director must discharge.
  3. Not Deductible to Company: Club subscriptions are explicitly not allowable business expenses under IRAS, meaning the company cannot deduct the interest foregone.
  4. Weak Business Justification: The stated purpose ("entertain clients and watch restaurant") can be achieved through:
    • Guest policies (non-members can attend as guests)
    • Non-member corporate entertaining arrangements
    • Cheaper alternatives (corporate venue hire, guest passes)
    • No personal membership is required
  5. Personal Asset Transfer: The membership is personal property of the director, not a corporate asset. Upon the director's death or departure, the company cannot recover the value, whereas a corporate investment would vest in the company.

This demonstrates poor judgment by directors in making what appears to be a disguised personal gift rather than a genuine business expense.
