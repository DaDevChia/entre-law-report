Rayfield v Hands

Citation[1960] Ch 1
CourtEngland and Wales (Chancery Division)
JurisdictionUnited Kingdom
Year1960
SourceLocal PDF: law_sources_documents/11/11. Rayfield v Hands.pdf

Background

Field-Davis Ltd was a private company carrying on business as builders and contractors, incorporated in 1941 under the Companies Act 1929, with a share capital of £4,000, divided into 4,000 ordinary shares of £1 each, of which 2,900 fully-paid shares had been issued. The plaintiff, Frank Leslie Rayfield, was the registered holder of 725 of those shares. The defendants, Gordon Wyndham Hands, Alfred William Scales, and Donald Davies, were at all material times the sole directors of the company.

The articles provided at Article 6: "No shares in the company shall be transferred to a person not a member of the company so long as any member of the company may be willing to purchase such shares at a fair value." Article 11 stated: "Every member who intends to transfer shares shall inform the directors who will take the said shares equally between them at a fair value but subject to the above no person shall hold more than 1,000 shares in the capital of the company." Article 12 provided that on the death of a director, his shares would be taken up by the remaining directors at a fair value.

On 4 April 1955, the plaintiff notified the defendants of his intention to transfer his 725 shares as provided by Article 11. The defendants denied any liability to take up and pay for the shares, contending that the articles imposed no enforceable liability upon them.

Key Holding

Vaisey J held that:

  1. On the true construction of Article 11, it created a binding obligation on the directors to purchase the plaintiff's shares at a fair price. The word "will" in "the directors who will take the said shares" indicated compulsion and obligation, not volition.
  2. Article 11 was concerned with the relationship between the plaintiff as a member and the defendants as members of the company (not in their capacity as directors). The obligation was therefore enforceable under s.20(1) of the Companies Act 1948 (equivalent to Singapore CA s.39(1)).
  3. It was not necessary for the plaintiff to join the company as a party to succeed in his action; he could enforce the articles directly against the defendants as members inter se.

The court applied the principle ut res magis valeat quam pereat: the articles of association, being a commercial document, should be construed to give them reasonable business efficacy, in preference to a result which would prove unworkable.

Ratio Decidendi

The articles of association (constitution) bind the company and its members, and also bind members inter se (between each other), pursuant to the statutory contract provision (UK CA s.20(1)/s.33(1); Singapore CA s.39(1)). Members can enforce provisions of the constitution directly against other members. Where an article imposes obligations on directors in their capacity as members (not qua directors), it is enforceable by other members under the statutory contract.

Relevance to Question 2 (Sea Paradise, Part A)

Directly relevant to the nature of Articles 10, 11, and 12:

  • The articles of Sea Paradise are a contract between the company and its members, and between members inter se (CA s.39(1)).
  • The promise made when inserting Articles 10-12 (that director interests would align with shareholders') is part of this constitutional contract.
  • The commercial unfairness doctrine builds on this contractual foundation: if the majority of directors/shareholders breach the legitimate expectations arising from the constitutional arrangement (Articles 10-12 and the promises made when they were inserted), there is commercial unfairness.
  • Shareholders who were told the articles would align interests and ensure dividends have a legitimate expectation of that outcome; its breach is cognisable under s.216.
