Re Sam Weller & Sons Ltd

Citation[1990] Ch 682
CourtEngland and Wales (Chancery Division)
JurisdictionUnited Kingdom
Year1990
SourceLocal PDF: law_sources_documents/5/5. Re Sam v Weller.pdf

Background

Sam Weller & Sons Ltd was a family company in Bradford, West Yorkshire, with an issued share capital of £18,000 in £1 shares. At the end of 1985, the company had net tangible assets of nearly £500,000, including cash of £216,969 and undistributed revenue profits of £464,623. The profit for that year was £36,330. Despite this, dividends had been maintained at 14 pence per share for some 37 years.

The company's sole director, Sam Weller ("S."), held 1,800 shares. His two sons Christopher and Anthony, employees of the company, each held 1,350 shares. The petitioners, James Weller (nephew) and Rosemary Sheppey (niece), held 2,450 and 450 shares respectively, totalling 7,700 shares (42.7% of issued capital) including shares they had become beneficially entitled to on the demise of other family shareholders.

The petitioners' complaints included: (a) the same payment of dividend for 37 years; (b) the company's purchase of a seaside flat without commercial justification; (c) a proposed capital expenditure of £130,000 without evidence it would prove profitable; and (d) the refusal to register shares to which they were entitled and to disclose the sole director's emoluments. S. and his sons applied to strike out the petition.

Key Holding

Peter Gibson J (judgment read 13 July 1989) dismissed the application to strike out the petition. The court held that:

  • The word "interests" in s.459(1) of the Companies Act 1985 is wider than "rights": members of a company may have different interests even if their rights as members are the same. A shareholder whose only return is from dividends has a different interest from a director-shareholder who also receives salary and emoluments.
  • Conduct can be unfairly prejudicial to the interests of some members even if it affects all members equally in terms of their rights, because members with the same rights may have different interests.
  • The test for unfair prejudice is objective: whether the company's affairs were being conducted in a manner unfairly prejudicial to the interests of some part of the members. Intention or motive of those responsible is irrelevant.
  • In the circumstances, the payment of low dividends (unchanged for 37 years) and the proposed capital expenditure of £130,000 were capable of amounting to conduct unfairly prejudicial to the interests of the petitioners.
  • The court declined to follow In re A Company (No. 00370 of 1987), Ex parte Glossop [1988] 1 W.L.R. 1068, which had held that inadequate dividends could not found a s.459 petition because all shareholders were affected equally. Peter Gibson J held that this was founded on an incorrect reading of s.459(1).

Ratio Decidendi

The failure to pay adequate dividends, when coupled with other conduct that advantages the majority shareholders at the expense of the minority, constitutes unfair prejudice/oppression under the minority protection provisions of the Companies Act. "Interests" in s.459 (equivalent to Singapore CA s.216) is broader than "rights," and members with the same rights may have different interests. The test is objective, not dependent on discriminatory intent.

Relevance to Question 2 (Sea Paradise, Part A)

This case establishes the principle that no or inadequate dividends + other unfair conduct = oppression. Applied to Sea Paradise:

  • The company has not paid dividends for 3 years despite making substantial profits.
  • Meanwhile, Director C receives an interest-free loan for a club membership, a personal benefit.
  • The combination of dividend withholding and director benefit parallels the Re Sam Weller pattern.
  • Provides authority that the 3-year dividend drought is itself a building block for the oppression claim, especially combined with the interest-free loan.
